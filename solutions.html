Finding Solutions

Tech arguments happen in almost every family. The good news is that most of them can be handled better with communication, trust, and a will to understand each other's POV.

Listen Before Responding

One of the fastest ways to make an argument worse is assuming you already know what the other person is thinking.

There's a famous saying: When you're arguing, the other side is usually preparing a comeback, not listening to your argument. DON'T MAKE THAT HAPPEN!

You might not agree with everything they say, but getting where they're coming from makes it much easier to solve the problem.

Talk About the Problem, Not the Person

There is a big difference between discussing a behavior and insulting someone.

Instead of: "You're addicted to games, you're just a failure!"

Try: "I'm worried because you've been playing half an hour of games every day, before homework."

One statement starts a conversation. The other usually starts an insult hurling contest.

Create Expectations Together

Rules often work better when everyone has a chance to discuss them.

Rather than one person making all the decisions, families can talk about expectations and find solutions that feel reasonable for everyone.

This could include things like:

  • Screen time limits
  • Homework before gaming
  • Phone free time
  • No phone use before bed

Focus on Balance

Tech isn't automatically good or bad.

Video games can be social and fun. Phones help people stay connected. The internet gives access to huge amounts of information.

At the same time, people still need sleep, exercise, responsibilities, and face to face interaction.

The goal is balance, not perfectionism.

Understand That Tech Means Different Things to Different People

For some parents, tech might mainly be a tool.

For many kids and teens, tech is also where friendships, hobbies, communities, and entertainment exist.

When people recognize that difference, conversations become much more useful.

Keep the Conversation Going

Tech changes quickly.

New games, apps, platforms, and memes appear all the time.

Because of this, tech discussions shouldn't be a one time thing. Families should keep talking, asking questions, and changing expectations when needed.

The goal isn't for parents to win or for kids to win. The goal is to build trust, understanding, and better habits that work for everyone.
